Who’s On the Other Side of Your Free Proxy?

The Dangers of Free Proxies and Free VPNs, and Why I’m Happy to Pay for My VPN By Jessie Nolan

Things You May Be Sharing with Third Parties Right Now:

  • Financial Information - Whenever you buy anything online, or log on to your online banking
  • Internet Activity - What you read, click on, search, email, which websites you visit
  • Who You Are - Your interests, your illnesses, your relationships, your thoughts, your fears
  • Where You Are - Right now, 10 minutes from now, yesterday, tomorrow, this summer
  • Information About Friends/Family - Everything you see or access online about them
  • Browsing Habits - When you use the Internet, what you use it for, how long you use it

Tired of the all unwanted surveillance? Start being safe online right now.
Still need persuading for some reason? Read on to learn why I’m happy to pay for my VPN.

When “Free” Isn’t Actually Free

The term “free proxy” or “free VPN” is misleading. Companies providing “free” proxies or VPNs aren’t just doing so out of the goodness of their hearts. In order to provide free or open proxies/VPNs, companies need to have large quantities of bandwidth—and that bandwidth is absolutely not free. So how are these companies paying for their bandwidth if they’re not charging their users?

Here’s Their Secret: They Profit Off of You.

How Free Proxy/VPN Companies Profit Off of You

Their solutions range from annoying inconveniences (random online advertisements, slow/unstable Internet connections), to using your data against you (targeted ads, spam), to sometimes something more sinister like stealing your confidential information (credit card or banking details, usernames and passwords, private photos).

By browsing the web unsecurely, you are allowing these types of companies to harvest your information and use it however they please.

“No matter whose network you’re using to go online, if you want to maximize your privacy... using a VPN (virtual private network) has become a viable option even for casual web users.” — Forbes

Why VPNs Should Cost Money

Maintaining servers is expensive. Finding and keeping talented people who are knowledgeable and care about security and online privacy is also expensive (and the right thing to do). Providing even the lowest level of customer experience still requires companies to pay up.

Think of it this way: the less money companies invest in their security and privacy products, the fewer resources they have to protect their users and servers against cyber criminal attacks or unlawful snooping.

Even companies like Forbes and Lifehacker are telling you to get a VPN already.

“You really should be using a VPN, and even if you don't think so now, at some point in the future you may consider it as important as your internet connection.” — Lifehacker

So... which VPN should I use?

I’ve tried several of the top paid VPNs on BestVPN.com, and they’re alright. I’ve been using ExpressVPN for over a year now, and they do exactly what I need them to. You should do your own digging, though.

The important thing is not settling for proxies or VPNs that claim to be “free”. Why compromise your online security and privacy just to save a few bucks a month?
